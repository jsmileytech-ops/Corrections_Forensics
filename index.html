The Environment

Incarcerated Physically.
Still Free Digitally.

Contraband phones have turned cells into command centers. Inmates run fraud, sextortion, drug logistics, and witness intimidation from inside, coordinating with networks on the outside in real time.

Smuggled smartphones are the single largest security and intelligence problem inside correctional facilities. They are used to direct criminal enterprises that never paused at the prison gate.

Documented cases include elaborate sextortion rings run entirely from inside maximum-security prisons, where incarcerated offenders extorted victims using banned devices that managed-access systems failed to stop.

Phones, drugs, and weapons increasingly arrive by drone, dropped over the wire at night. Interdiction vendors have already added drone detection to their managed-access offerings, an admission that the airspace is now the perimeter.

Detection alone only confirms a drone was present. The intelligence, who flew it and from where, lives in the recovered aircraft's data.

No contraband phone smuggles itself. Behind every device is a supplier, a courier, a financier, and a coordinator on the outside. The inside actor is one visible node in a network that operates across the wall.

Today that network is rarely mapped, because the forensic value of a seized device usually stops at the device.

When an individual is released to probation, the facility prepares a reentry package summarizing what is known about them. Today that package rarely reflects the criminal network the person was connected to while inside.

The relationships that pull people back into crime are exactly the relationships a contraband-phone investigation already surfaced, if anyone connected the dots.

The gap

Facilities spend heavily to detect and block contraband devices, yet the seized devices and downed drones, the richest intelligence in the building, are forensically underused. The data is collected and then left on a shelf.

The Force Multiplier

The Lead Doesn't
Stop at the Fence

Connect three data sources around a single incident and a contraband seizure becomes a street-level investigation.

Source A

Seized phone

Contacts, messengers, cloud accounts, and the financial trail of the operation, extracted by Oxygen.

Source B

Recovered drone

Flight path, home point, and operator data, pointing to a launch location and a person.

Source C

ALPR in the lot

Plate-reader data from visitor and perimeter lots places vehicles, and people, at the facility at the right time.

The correlation

A phone contact who is also a plate captured in the lot during a drone-drop window is not a coincidence. It is a lead, with a name, a vehicle, and a location, handed to police outside the wall. Criminals on the inside are best friends with criminals on the outside; this is how you map that friendship.

Lawful use, built in (this is a strength, not a footnote)

Visitor lots hold families, children, and attorneys. Any outside-the-wall capability must be warrant-backed, minimized, audit-logged, and tied to articulable predicate, never a dragnet on visitors. Defensibility by design is what makes this sellable to general counsel and survivable in court. It is also where deep FAR/DFARS and evidence-handling fluency separates this pitch from a naive one.
